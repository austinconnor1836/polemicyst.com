Most fans feel this in real time: a lower seed steals Game 1 or Game 2 on the road, and suddenly everyone says, "Series changed."

That instinct is correct. In seven-game playoff formats, one away win can flip the practical home-court advantage.

## Start with the standard 2-2-1-1-1 setup

In the NBA and NHL, the higher seed hosts Games 1, 2, 5, and 7.  
The lower seed hosts Games 3, 4, and 6.

Before the series starts, the higher seed's edge is simple:

- They own four possible home games instead of three.
- If both teams "hold serve" (win every home game), the higher seed wins Game 7 at home.

That is what home-court advantage means in practice: **you get the tiebreaker location.**

## The flip happens when the road team steals one

Suppose the lower seed wins Game 1 on the road.

Now imagine each team wins every remaining home game:

- Lower seed already has one road win (Game 1).
- Lower seed also wins its home games (Games 3, 4, 6).
- Series ends in 6 with the lower seed advancing.

The higher seed can no longer rely on "just hold home court and survive."  
They now must steal a road game to cancel out the one they gave away.

That is the flip:

> Home-court advantage is no longer a higher-seed privilege.  
> It becomes a race to reclaim one road game.

## Why this feels bigger than one game

A single game result does two things at once:

1. It gives the road team a win.
2. It transfers control of the "hold serve" script.

After a road steal, the lower seed can often reduce the series to a cleaner formula:

- "Take care of home, then close in 6."

Meanwhile, the higher seed moves from control to chase mode:

- "Must respond on the road or force a Game 7 under stress."

## This logic applies beyond the NBA

Any seven-game format with uneven home allocation has the same structure.

- **NHL (2-2-1-1-1):** exact same flip dynamic as the NBA.
- **MLB postseason rounds (commonly 2-3-2):** still a flip, just with a different travel rhythm.

In all cases, a road win by the lower-seeded team changes who can "win by holding home."

## The quick rule of thumb

If you only remember one line, use this:

**In a 7-game series, the first road win usually transfers leverage.**

Not because momentum is mystical.  
Because the math of the home/away schedule changed.

One stolen road game turns home-court advantage from an asset into a problem to solve.
